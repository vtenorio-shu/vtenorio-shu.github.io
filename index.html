About

Engineer by training, scientist by obsession

I grew up in Brazil asking questions about how systems work, then spent the next decade building them: first as an electrical engineer working on IoT security and AI products, then as a neuroscientist studying how the brain generates movement. What ties it all together is a genuine love for understanding complex systems and making them do something useful.

My PhD at Newcastle University, funded by a Marie Curie Fellowship (1 position from roughly 100 applicants), focused on the motor system of awake, behaving primates. I was among the first researchers to simultaneously record high-density neural activity from primary motor cortex and the brainstem reticular formation, showing that information flows in both directions, not just top-down, and that the rotational dynamics people thought were unique to cortex also show up deeper in the brain. I did this while managing international collaborations across France, Greece, and the UK, and building the entire data pipeline from scratch.

Outside the lab, I have shipped AI products, secured IoT systems, built hardware at a cybersecurity startup, and advised early-stage companies on security architecture. I am fluent in Portuguese, English and Italian, and I genuinely enjoy working across disciplines, cultures and time zones. For the full story, find me on LinkedIn.

Research

What I work on

My research lives at the crossroads of systems neuroscience, dynamical systems theory, and information theory. The big question: how do distributed brain circuits actually coordinate to produce movement?

🧠

Cortical and subcortical motor dynamics

Simultaneous high-density recordings from primary motor cortex and the brainstem reticular formation in behaving primates. Information flows in both directions, and rotational dynamics show up at both levels of the motor hierarchy.

📡

Information-theoretic analysis of neural data

Transfer entropy, interaction information, and directed connectivity measures applied to high-dimensional spike trains. The goal is understanding what information moves where, and when, during voluntary movement.

🌀

Dynamical systems and population geometry

jPCA, dimensionality reduction, and latent dynamical models that reveal low-dimensional structure hiding in high-dimensional neural population activity. What does movement look like as a trajectory through neural state space?

⚡

Neural data infrastructure

Building the pipelines that make large-scale electrophysiology actually usable. Multi-area Neuropixels recordings, reproducible analysis workflows, and open datasets as part of the IN2PrimateBrains consortium.

🔗

Network modelling in neurological disorders

Earlier work characterising functional and effective connectivity during absence seizures, in collaboration with Hospital Israelita Albert Einstein in Brazil. Network theory applied to clinical EEG data.

🔧

Hardware and embedded systems

FPGA programming, embedded systems design, IoT security and zero-trust architecture. The engineering background that means I can talk to hardware people and neuroscientists in the same afternoon.
